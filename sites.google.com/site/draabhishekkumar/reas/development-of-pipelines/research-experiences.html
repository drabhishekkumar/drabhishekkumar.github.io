Research Experiences

·      M. Sc.

After completing my B.Sc. with subjects Zoology, Botany and Chemistry, I studied and gain a year of experience in at School of Environmental Sciences, Jawaharlal Nehru University, New Delhi and learned about basic and advanced theories and practical associated with Environment Science and Ecology. Then, I was very fortunate to continue my post-graduate study in Biotechnology at School of Biotechnology, Madurai Kamaraj University, a premier research institution in India. The various theoretical and practical courses that I have been involved in here have equipped me to enter the world of scientific research with poise and confidence. At the end of first year M. Sc, I took up a summer project entitled "The Thermal Effects on Biomolecular Systems: Molecular Modeling and Simulational Studies" at Centre for Biotechnology, Jawaharlal Nehru University, New Delhi, India in Molecular Modeling of proteins. I examined the effect of temperature on proteins by using molecular modeling & simulational studies. This was a novel experience and I really enjoyed mastering the different computational approaches of molecular modeling. In December 2000 through an online test, I got opportunity to work in Computational Neurobiology at National Centre for Biological Sciences, Bangalore, India. I did a small project entitled as “Modeling of Extracellular signal-Regulated Kinase (ERK) Signaling Pathways in Neurons” that gave me idea how to model a signaling pathway and its kinetics & how to solve a problem related to that pathway. I was involved in an independent research project entitled "Structural analysis of outer membrane beta-stranded porins using Temperature factor" at Bioinformatics Centre, Madurai Kamaraj University, India.

 In this project, I wrote scripts for calculating average atomic temperature factors (B-values) and its standard deviations for different secondary structures at atom type level and residue type level of high resolution crystal structures of porins and found that inner wall of porins that face towards trimmer interface has lower average B-values than that of outer wall and residue distributions in all porins is quite different different (Kumar & Krishnaswamy (2008), Nature Precedings).

Recently a review on porin became available as (Kumar et al. (2014), Protein and Peptide Letters).

·      Ph.D. - “Phylogenomics of vertebrate serpins”

I worked at the University of Bielefeld as Ph.D. researcher and I have been dealing with comparative and phylogenetic analysis of serine proteinase inhibitors (serpins). The serpins constitute a superfamily of proteins that fold into a conserved tertiary structure and employ a sophisticated, irreversible suicide-mechanism of inhibition. More than 500 serpins have been identified, occurring in all three forms of the life- the eukaryotes, the prokaryotes and the archea. Vertebrate serpins can be conveniently classified into six groups, V1-V6, based on three independent biological features - genomic organization, diagnostic amino acid sites and rare indels. The present vertebrate serpins are derived from an original serpin most probably by intron insertion. I have investigated the phylogenetic relationship of serpin from Nematostella vectensis, Strongylocentrotus purpuratus, Drosophila melanogaster, Ciona intestinalis, six fish species, frog, chicken and mammals using gene architecture analyses and stringent criteria for identification of orthologs. All vertebrate serpin genes fit into one of the six exon/intron gene classes previously identified with minor deviations, dating the existence and maintenance of these gene organizations before or close to the divergence of fishes. Group V1 and V2 gene families underwent rapid adaptive radiation along the lineages leading to mammals as indicated by an up to nine-fold increase of family members, accompanied by a rapid functional diversification. In contrast, gene groups V3 to V6 display a rather conservative evolution with little changes since the divergence of fishes and the other vertebrates. There is no indication for a duplication of serpins genes indicative of fish-specific whole genome duplication events. Alternatively duplicated genes may have been lost. The orthology assessment indicates that all vertebrates are equipped with a subset of strongly conserved serpins with functions that can be clearly correlated with basic vertebrate-specific physiology. 

Amongst the vertebrate serpins the genomic organisation and corresponding syntenies between different genomes were also analysed 

and the result show massive gene duplication, gene diversification, and gene gain and loss during vertebrate evolution over 450 Mya. The urochordates and invertebrates do not share even a single genome synteny level conservation. None of Ciona intestinalis serpin genes shares a common exon-intron architecture organisation with any of the vertebrate serpin gene classes, nor was it possible to identify orthologs of vertebrates. The lack of gene architecture similarity and the complete absence of orthology between urochordate and vertebrate serpin indicate that major changes with bursts of character acquisition must have occurred during evolution of serpins in the time interval separating urochordates from chordates, indicating massive intron gain/loss and events providing C and N-terminal sequence extensions characteristic for today’s vertebrate serpins.

On unravelling the comparative genomics of serpins into evolutionary sense, I found two major outcomes of research work as following:

Rare genomic characters are used to show that orthologs of neuroserpin, a prominent representative of vertebrate group V3 serpin genes, exist in early diverging deuterostomes and probably also in cnidarians, indicating that the origin of a mammalian serpin can be traced back far in the history of eumetazoans. A C-terminal address code assigning association with secretory pathway organelles is present in all neuroserpin orthologs, suggesting that supervision of cellular export/import routes by antiproteolytic serpins is an ancient trait  (Kumar & Ragg (2008), BMC Evolutionary Biology).

 

After establishment of canonical exon-intron patterns in the serpins superfamily at the dawn of vertebrate evolution, multiple intron acquisition events have occurred during diversification of a lineage of actinopterygian fishes. The novel introns were gathered within a limited time interval (on an evolutionary timescale), and no such events were observed in other groups of vertebrates. Examination of the sequences flanking the intron insertion points revealed that the genetic requirements for acquisition of novel introns may be less stringent than previously suggested. Finally, we argue that genome compaction, a phenomenon associated with the fish lineage depicting preferential intron gain, might promote intron gain (Ragg & Kumar et al. (2009), BMC Evolutionary Biology).

 


·      Understanding mechanisms of human diseases

During this Ph.D. period, I gained experiences in evolutionary genomics and biology and my quest for applying comparative genomics approaches to different medicinal issues enormously got enhanced as I realized that comparative approaches could unravel newer understandings into different human diseases and disorders. To carry on further, I have been involved into two different projects as described below:

·      Unraveling novel human kinases and their potential roles in human diseases such as cancer

 In collaboration, cardiovascular research group in University of Munich (LMU), I found that the previously published coding sequence of the STK35 gene is incomplete. The newly identified STK35 gene codes for a protein of 534 amino acids. This protein (but not STK35/Clik1) was found to be expressed in all human cells studied (endothelial cells, Macrophages, HeLa and HEK cells) EGFP-STK35L was localized in the nucleus and the nucleolus. Hence I termed this protein as STK35L1 (STK35 Long). By combining syntenic and gene structure pattern data and homology searches, a new STK35L protein kinase sub-family consisting of three members (STK35L1, STK35L2, and STK35L3) was identified. Interestingly, all these members were conserved throughout the vertebrates. However, STK35L3 was specifically lost during placental mammalian evolution. Using comparative genomics, we have identified orthologous sets of these three genes and their possible ancestor gene in two sea squirt genomes. We have characterized a new subfamily of kinases termed as ‘STK35L subfamily’. The localization of STK35L1 in cells suggests a functional role in the nucleolus (Goyal et al. (2009), PLoS ONE and Goyal et al. (2011), PLoS ONE). This study will give a platform to explore the role of STK35L kinases in cell functions and human diseases.

  Comparative genomics of cerebral cavernous malformations (CCM) and unraveling pathways in different model organisms

The three cerebral cavernous malformations (CCM) genes namely CCM1 (KRIT1), CCM2 (MGC4607), and CCM3 (PDCD10) have been identified, which by mutation cause cerebral cavernous malformations, a common deadly disease in humans. Defining the molecular phylogeny of CCM genes by using comparative genomics tools is one approach to understand the different links amongst their gene structure, synteny organization and their function in these biological processes, which are not fully understood yet. The CCM genes first appear in invertebrates and our studies suggest that the regulation and biological actions of this gene are preserved across invertebrates to vertebrates. This comprehensive analysis of the CCM genes across phyla provides new information that will aid further characterization of these CCM genes in their biological processes which will let new era of understanding in the cerebral cavernous malformations as a disease and their medicinal aspect. Understanding basic pathway in different model organisms is necessary in order to provide answers of unsolved mechanisms controlling this disease.

·      Comparative genomics of GPCRs such as  lysophosphatidic acid receptors (LPARs) and melanocortin receptors (MCR)

 

GPCRs forms largest superfamily of transmembrane proteins and these serve as >30% of pharmaceutically important receptors. Herein, I studied the comparative genomics of - lysophosphatidic acid receptor (LPA) receptors and melanocortin (MC) receptors. I unraveled the molecular phylogeny of these GPCRs and I found that these genes have their own evolutionary history such as gain /loss of novel introns in genome compacted fishes. Part of results, I have published as Kumar et al. (2011, PLoS ONE).



·      Molecular Phylogeney of OVO-like transcription factors

  

               I designed and carried out molecular phylogeny of OVO-like transcription factors. It was reported that a core domain is conserved 

from early-branching metazoa to humans and it is comprising a tetrad of C2H2 zinc fingers. By domain comparison of the OVOL proteins, this study found that they evolved in different metazoan lineages by attaching intrinsically-disordered (ID) segments of N/C-terminal extensions of 100 to 1000 amino acids to this conserved core.

These ID regions originated independently across different animal lineages giving rise to different types of OVOL genes over the course of metazoan evolution. This analysis illustrated the molecular evolution of metazoan OVOL genes over a period of 700 million years (MY). This study both extends our current understanding of the structure/function relationship of metazoan OVOL genes, and assembles a good platform for further characterization of OVOL genes from diverged organisms (Kumar et al. (2012), PLoS ONE).

·      Research Associate at University of Kiel

o   Genome sequencing, assembly and annotation of marine fungi for bio- and chemical diversity

It is estimated that there are several hundreds of marine fungi species and they possess unique features in the fungal diversity, as they have to adopt marine environment. The enormous biodiversity of marine fungi is mirrored by the molecular diversity of their secondary metabolites. However, a very little is known about marine fungal genetic resources. Although several terrestrial habitant fungal genomes have been sequenced using Sanger sequencing in last two decades and recently, the Sordaria macrospora genomic sequencing became available using next-generation sequencing. However, not a single genome of marine isolates of fungi is yet known. I have established the genome and transcriptomes of three marine fungi (namely Scopulariopsis brevicaulis, Pestalotiopsis and Calcarisporium) using three different next-generation sequencing methods namely, Roche 454, Illumina and Ion torrent. This genome characterization assists mycologist to further carry out research with this species, which largely hindered due to unavailability of the genome. There are four manuscripts in preparation from these research works. Seven potential drug-like molecules are identified during this work, including one anti-cancerous bioactive molecule. These molecules are under consideration to sell to pharmaceutical companies.

 

o   Studying unknown gene bem46 from Neurospora crassa

Gene annotation and characterizations are still challenging tasks. These lead into problems for genome sequencing projects and about 40-50% of genes in genome remains unknown till today. Some of these genes are widespread conserved and termed as “known unknown“ and bem46 gene is such a gene, which belongs the alpha/beta-hydrolase superfamily. In desktop genomic era, it is critical to find solution for large dataset that remain unknown. Recently, we demonstrated how conserved BEM46 homologs are evolved by using exonic indel during eukaryotic evolution. The gene strucutures of BEM46 homologs are hypervarible with one exon in yeast to thirteen exon in human. We illustrated the catalytic sites of BEM46 Neurospora crassa as a catalytic triad formed by a serine 188S, a histidine 292H and an aspartic acid 262D. We also characterized bem46 gene from N. crassa for localization studies and we defined new ER-retention signal “PEKK”, which was previously not described (Kumar et al. (2013b), BBRC). Such studies are urgent requirements in modern biosciences to study new genes. Otherwise, we will remain puzzling about half of genes in newly sequenced genomes using rapid sequencing technologies.

 

o   RNA secondary structure changes with RNA editing events

The maize cox2 RNA is fully edited in cauliflower mitochondria. Deletions in the cox2 RNA sequence can have a strong effect on RNA processing, leading to loss of splicing, loss of editing at all sites, or even to a gain of new editing sites. As these effects are not limited to the vicinity of the respective deletions, but appear to be widespread or even affect all editing sites, they may not be explained by the loss of PPR binding sites. Instead, it appears that several parts of the cox2 transcript are required for proper RNA processing. This indicates the roles of the RNA sequence and structural elements in the recognition of the editing sites (Gruettner et al. (2014), PLoS ONE).
